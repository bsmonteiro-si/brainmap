## Assistant

Pearl is making a deep point here, and it has a few layers worth unpacking.

## The core claim: causal structure is more stable than statistical patterns

Imagine a causal diagram for smallpox vaccination:

```
Hygiene ──→ Smallpox Risk
Vaccination ──→ Immune Response ──→ Smallpox Risk
Vaccine Safety ──→ Side Effects
```

Now change the world dramatically — better hygiene, a safer vaccine, lower baseline disease risk. **Every probability changes.** P(smallpox), P(side effects), P(benefit | vaccinated) — all different numbers now.

But the *arrows* don't change. Vaccination still causes an immune response. Hygiene still affects disease risk. The causal *mechanism* is the same even though the statistical *magnitudes* are completely different.

## Why this matters practically

When you do causal analysis, you derive an **estimand** — a formula like "to estimate the effect of X on Y, adjust for Z and compute this specific quantity from data." That formula comes from the *structure* of the diagram (which variables are confounders, which are mediators, etc.), not from the specific numbers.

So if the diagram stays the same but you get new data from the changed world, you just plug the new data into the same formula. You don't have to redo the reasoning about *what to adjust for* or *how to decompose the effect*. The recipe transfers; only the ingredients change.

A purely statistical model — say, a regression fit on old data — would be useless in the new world. The coefficients would be wrong, and you'd have no principled way to know *which parts* to update.

## The philosophical punch

Pearl is arguing this isn't just a technical convenience — it reflects something about how human cognition works. We think in terms of "smoking causes cancer" rather than "P(cancer | smoking) = 0.17." The causal statement is portable across contexts (different populations, time periods, countries). The probability is not.

His conjecture is that evolution favored causal reasoning precisely *because* of this robustness. An organism that learns "fire causes burns" can apply that knowledge in any new environment. An organism that learns "probability of burn in this specific savanna = 0.3" has fragile knowledge that breaks the moment conditions change.

In short: **causal knowledge compresses the world into reusable, transferable structure, while statistical knowledge is a snapshot bound to the conditions that produced it.**
