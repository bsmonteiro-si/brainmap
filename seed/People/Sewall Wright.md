# Sewall Wright

Sewall Wright (1889-1988) was an American geneticist who developed path analysis — the first systematic method for representing causal relationships using directed diagrams. Working at the USDA and later at the University of Chicago, Wright applied his method to problems in genetics, particularly the inheritance of coat color in guinea pigs.

## Path Analysis

Wright's key innovation was the use of directed diagrams with arrows connecting variables to represent hypothesized causal influences. Each arrow carried a "path coefficient" quantifying the strength of the causal effect. The diagram as a whole encoded a complete causal model that could decompose observed correlations into direct and indirect causal pathways.

## Rejection and Vindication

The statistical establishment, still under the influence of Karl Pearson's anti-causal philosophy, rejected Wright's approach. His path diagrams were dismissed as unscientific speculation — arrows representing causal claims were seen as going beyond what data could support. This rejection kept path analysis confined to genetics for decades. It was only with Pearl's formalization in the 1990s that Wright's fundamental insight — that directed graphs could serve as a formal language for causal assumptions — was vindicated and generalized into the framework of causal diagrams and the do-calculus.
