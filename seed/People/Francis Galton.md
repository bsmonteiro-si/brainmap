# Francis Galton

Sir Francis Galton (1822-1911) was a Victorian polymath whose investigations into heredity led to the discovery of two foundational concepts in statistics: regression to the mean and correlation. His work shaped the trajectory of statistical science for over a century, though it also set the stage for the anti-causal orthodoxy that would follow.

## Contributions to Statistics

Galton's study of hereditary height revealed that tall parents tend to have children closer to the population average — a phenomenon he called "regression toward mediocrity." This insight was revolutionary: it showed that extreme values in one generation do not persist unchanged into the next. He also developed the concept of correlation as a measure of association between two variables, providing the mathematical foundation for Karl Pearson's later work.

## Limitations and Legacy

Galton struggled with the direction of causation in his findings. His tools could quantify associations but could not distinguish cause from effect or account for confounding variables. This limitation was inherited by his intellectual successors, particularly Karl Pearson, who elevated the inability to reason causally into a philosophical principle. Galton's path diagrams and visual thinking, however, presaged the graphical approaches that would eventually enable formal causal reasoning.
