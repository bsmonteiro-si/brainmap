# Ronald Fisher

Sir Ronald Aylmer Fisher (1890-1962) was one of the most influential statisticians of the 20th century, whose contributions include the analysis of variance (ANOVA), maximum likelihood estimation, and the randomized controlled trial. His legacy in causal inference is deeply paradoxical: he invented the most powerful causal tool in science while simultaneously opposing formal causal reasoning.

## The Randomized Controlled Trial

Fisher introduced randomization into experimental design at Rothamsted Experimental Station in the 1920s. His insight was that randomly assigning treatments to experimental units eliminates systematic bias and provides a valid basis for statistical inference. The RCT became the gold standard for establishing causal effects in medicine, agriculture, and the social sciences.

## The Smoking Paradox

Despite inventing a fundamentally causal methodology, Fisher resisted formal causal language in statistical analysis. Most notoriously, he argued against the causal interpretation of the association between smoking and lung cancer, suggesting that a genetic confounding factor could explain the correlation. Without formal causal tools (which did not exist in his time), his objection could not be rigorously evaluated. Pearl later showed that a properly specified causal diagram would have resolved the debate decades earlier, potentially saving millions of lives. Fisher's case illustrates both the consequences of lacking formal causal tools and the irony of a scientist who created causal methodology while rejecting causal reasoning.
