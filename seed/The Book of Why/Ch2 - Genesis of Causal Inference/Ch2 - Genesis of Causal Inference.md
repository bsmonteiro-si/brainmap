# Ch2 - Genesis of Causal Inference

Pearl traces the intellectual lineage of causal thinking through four pivotal figures in the history of statistics. The story begins with Francis Galton, who discovered regression to the mean and correlation while studying heredity. Galton's insight that tall parents tend to have shorter children (and vice versa) was groundbreaking, but he struggled with the direction of causation. His tools could describe associations but not explain why they existed.

Karl Pearson, Galton's intellectual heir, took this limitation and elevated it into a principle. He argued that science should restrict itself to describing correlations, never claiming to explain causes. This anti-causal philosophy dominated statistics for decades, creating a disciplinary taboo against causal language that delayed the development of formal causal methods by nearly a century.

The exception to this orthodoxy was Sewall Wright, a geneticist who developed path analysis in the 1920s. Wright used directed diagrams with arrows to represent causal relationships in his studies of guinea pig genetics, essentially inventing the first causal DAGs decades before Pearl. The statistical establishment rejected his approach, but Wright's work survived in genetics and was eventually recognized as a direct precursor to modern causal inference. Ronald Fisher, despite inventing the randomized controlled trial, paradoxically resisted formal causal language and famously argued against the causal link between smoking and lung cancer.
